FOR many car buyers, the aroma of a new car can be a deal breaker. New car smell -- an amalgam of odors from the rubber, leather, cloth, plastic and chemicals used in making the car -- can draw buyers to a car the way an animal is drawn to its kin. Or it can turn them off. If the smell reminds them of a wicked aunt's old Buick or gives them the sense that they are losing brain cells from the chemicals, they may change their buying decision.  Which is why manufacturers go to great lengths to cultivate a signature smell. They treat the leather and the upholstery and sometimes add aromas, with luck that the bouquet may spark that sensor in the buyer's nose and send a message to his brain that says, ''Buy.''
